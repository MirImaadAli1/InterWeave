UI/UX Design

Good and bad practices of UX design

  • Lynn Aranha
  • January 14, 2024
Share

Introduction:

User Experience (UX) design plays a pivotal role in shaping our interactions with digital products. From seamless navigation to visually pleasing interfaces, the impact of UX is profound. In this blog post, we'll explore examples of both good and bad UX design, uncovering the principles that elevate user experiences or leave users frustrated.

The Anatomy of Good UX Design

  1. Intuitive Navigation:

    Good UX design prioritizes intuitive navigation. Users should effortlessly find what they're looking for. A prime example is the navigation menu on Airbnb's website, offering straightforward categories, making the user journey smooth and efficient.

  2. Responsive Design:

    Websites and applications that adapt seamlessly to various devices exemplify good UX. Google's Material Design is a shining example, providing a consistent experience across platforms, from smartphones to desktops.

  3. Clear Call-to-Action (CTA):

    Effective CTAs guide users through a digital experience. Slack's use of a prominently placed "Join Now" button with clear, concise text is an excellent example. The color contrast and placement make the CTA instantly noticeable.

  4. Feedback Mechanisms:

    Users should be informed about their actions. Gmail's undo feature is a brilliant example. After sending an email, users receive a brief window to undo the action, preventing accidental sends and providing a sense of control.

  5. Accessibility:

    Designs that consider diverse user needs showcase good UX. Apple's accessibility features, such as VoiceOver for the visually impaired, demonstrate a commitment to inclusive design, ensuring everyone can use their products.

Learning from Bad UX Design

  1. Confusing Layouts:

    Complex and cluttered interfaces confuse users. A classic example is the early design of Microsoft's Clippy in Microsoft Office, which disrupted workflows with unsolicited assistance, leading to frustration.

  2. Hidden Information:

    Hiding crucial information frustrates users. When Facebook buried privacy settings in a complex menu, users struggled to control their privacy, resulting in a negative user experience.

  3. Inconsistent Design:

    Inconsistency disrupts the flow of user interactions. Windows 8 received criticism for its inconsistent interface, with a mix of traditional desktop elements and new touch-centric designs, causing confusion.

  4. Lack of Feedback:

    Users need feedback for their actions. Forms with unclear error messages or no feedback, like those encountered in some poorly designed e-commerce sites, leave users in the dark about their input errors.

  5. Slow Performance:

    Slow-loading websites or applications frustrate users. A notorious example is the early rollout of the Healthcare.gov website, which suffered from sluggish performance, impacting user experience during a critical time.

Conclusion:

In the dynamic world of digital design, learning from both positive and negative examples is crucial. By analyzing instances of good and bad UX design, designers can refine their practices, creating digital experiences that delight users and stand the test of time. Striking the balance between innovation and user-centricity is the key to crafting memorable and effective UX designs.
